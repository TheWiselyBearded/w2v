World2Vision — teaching a machine to see the sky.

A textbook for building computer-vision systems that detect and track small, fast-moving objects in the air — drones, aircraft, birds, balloons, and other aerial vehicles — starting from an empty repository and a camera pointed at nothing but clouds. No background in machine learning is assumed. Every term is defined the first time it appears.

what the world actually contains — ground truth what the model thinks it sees — a detection

The whole of this book lives in the gap between those two boxes. When the green box and the red box sit on top of each other, you have a working perception system. When they drift apart — a bird called a drone, a mountain ridge called an aircraft, a real object the model never saw at all — you have the actual subject of the field: the discipline of making a model's beliefs match reality, and measuring, honestly, how far apart they still are.

§Who this is for

It is written for the engineer who can program but has never trained a model; for the ML practitioner who has trained models on tidy academic datasets and now faces the mess of a real sensor; and for the curious reader who wants to know how a small aerial vehicle is taught to notice another one before they meet. The running example is air-to-air detection — a camera on a flying platform looking for other things that fly — because it concentrates almost every hard problem in computer vision into a single frame: objects a dozen pixels wide, backgrounds full of clutter, motion, and a cost to being wrong.

◇ A note on scope

This is a book about perception — turning images into detections, tracks, and honest uncertainty. It is not a book about what a system does with those detections. The same techniques that let a delivery drone avoid a passing aircraft let an airspace monitor count birds near a wind farm or a research platform log traffic; the perception layer is shared. We draw the line at the detection and stop there, on purpose — and Chapter 2 explains why drawing that line in code, not just in prose, is itself an engineering decision.

§How to read it

Front to back, ideally. The chapters form one arc rather than a reference manual, and each leans on the one before it. But the four parts are self-contained enough that you can drop into the one you need:

  • Part I — The problem & the shape. Why finding a drone in the sky is hard, and the architecture that makes the rest of the book possible.
  • Part II — Manufacturing truth. Where labeled data comes from when you cannot buy it: building synthetic worlds, deriving labels from geometry, and versioning datasets so they never quietly lie to you.
  • Part III — Learning to see. Choosing and training a detector, and then the central drama of the whole field — the gap between the simulator and the real sky, and the tricks that close it.
  • Part IV — Judgment & the loop. Measuring what actually matters, getting the model off your desk and onto the aircraft, and closing the loop so the system teaches itself what to learn next.

Throughout, the failures stay in. The chapter where a flipped camera vector silently mislabels an entire dataset teaches as much as any success, and honest numbers — a model that scores 0.02 on the wrong benchmark and 0.92 on the right one — are the point, not an embarrassment to be smoothed over.
