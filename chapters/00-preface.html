How to read this book

A short orientation: what this is, what it is not, why it teaches through a single hard example, and why every failure was left on the page.

Most computer-vision tutorials hand you a folder of cat photos and a model that already works. You run three commands, watch an accuracy number climb, and learn almost nothing about why the number climbed or what you would do the day it doesn't. This book takes the opposite bet. It follows one genuinely hard problem — finding a small flying object from a moving camera — all the way from an empty directory to a model running on the vehicle, and it refuses to skip the parts where things break.

The choice of example is deliberate. A drone two kilometers away is roughly twenty pixels tall. It moves against a background of cloud, terrain, and glare. Sometimes it is a bird. Sometimes the bird is the thing you care about and the drone is the distraction. The camera itself is moving, so the whole world slides across the frame. Nearly every difficulty that makes real computer vision hard — tiny objects, domain shift, class confusion, the tyranny of false alarms — is present in a single frame of this problem. Master it here and the rest of the field feels familiar.

§The one idea to keep

If you remember nothing else, remember this: the pipeline is the product. Newcomers fixate on the model — which architecture, how many layers, whose pretrained weights. Practitioners learn that the model is the most swappable part of the whole system. What actually determines whether your detector works in the sky is everything around the model: where the data came from, whether the labels are correct, whether your test set is honest, whether the thing you measured on your laptop is the thing that matters on the aircraft. Ten of this book's twelve chapters are about that surrounding machinery, because that is where the real engineering, and the real failures, live.

▸ How the chapters are shaped

Each chapter opens with the problem in plain language before any code appears, builds the solution one decision at a time, and closes with a short list of takeaways you could repeat to a colleague. Technical terms are set in bold with a dotted underline the first time they appear, and defined on the spot. You should never have to already know the answer to understand the question.

§Why the failures stay in

Real systems are built by making mistakes and noticing them. A tutorial that shows only the clean path teaches you to recognize success but not to diagnose failure — and diagnosis is the job. So the war stories are here in full. You will read about a single flipped vector that mislabeled an entire synthetic dataset in a way that looked perfectly consistent until one absolute test caught it. You will read about a benchmark that reported a catastrophic 0.02 when the true number was 0.92, because the test set was a single pathological flight. These are not cautionary asides. They are the curriculum.

When you see the red bar, slow down: there is a lesson in it that no amount of correct code would have taught.

§What you need to bring

You should be comfortable reading code — the examples are in Python, and you should not be frightened by a class definition or a command line. You do not need any machine learning, any computer graphics, or any knowledge of aircraft. Where the book needs a piece of math — a camera projection, an intersection-over-union, an average precision — it builds the idea from the ground up rather than citing it. The goal is that you finish able to build a system like this, not merely to admire one.

§A word on the domain

The running example — a flying camera looking for other flying things — is a real and consequential problem. The same perception stack serves a delivery drone that must see and avoid crossing air traffic, an airport that must track birds near a runway, a research platform cataloguing wildlife, and the broad family of autonomous aerial vehicles, from hobbyist quadcopters to fast, uncrewed craft, that increasingly share the sky and must be able to notice one another. This book is about the seeing. What any particular system does after it sees is a separate question with its own hard constraints, and — as Chapter 2 argues — one worth keeping firmly on the other side of a line you draw in the code itself.

Takeaways
  • The pipeline is the product. The model is the swappable part; the machinery around it decides whether the system works.
  • One hard example beats ten easy ones. Air-to-air detection concentrates most of computer vision's real difficulties into a single frame.
  • Failures are the curriculum. Every war story in this book happened, and each one teaches something a success could not.
  • No prerequisites, no hand-waving. Every term is defined where it appears; every piece of math is built, not cited.
