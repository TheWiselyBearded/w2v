Part III · Chapter 8

Tiny Objects & Tiling — keeping a twenty-pixel target alive all the way to the network.

A detector has a fixed, modest input resolution. A real sensor frame is enormous. Naively reconciling the two destroys exactly the small objects you built the whole system to catch. This chapter is about the single most effective technique for small-object detection — and the runtime bill it hands you.

Chapter 1 promised that small objects would demand their own chapter, and Chapter 7 named the culprit in the sim-to-real accounting: a resolution mismatch between what your model expects and what the sensor delivers. This is the chapter where we fix it. The fix is conceptually simple, easy to get subtly wrong, and the most expensive thing your runtime will do. Let us earn all three of those claims.

8.1 The object is destroyed before layer one

Object detectors take a fixed input size. Depending on the architecture that might be 560 or 640 pixels on a side — a deliberate compromise between accuracy and speed baked into the network when it was designed. Your sensor, meanwhile, produces something far larger: call it 2448 × 2048, a perfectly ordinary five-megapixel frame.

Now do the obvious thing. Hand the whole frame to the detector. To fit a 2448-pixel-wide image into a 560-pixel input, the framework must shrink it by a factor of roughly 4.4×. Every dimension in the image shrinks with it — including the object. That twenty-pixel aircraft from Chapter 1 is now four or five pixels across. Four pixels is below the floor at which the network can resolve anything at all. There is no feature to extract, no edge to find, no gradient that means "aircraft." The object was not missed by the detector. It was erased by the preprocessing, before the first convolution ever ran.

Downscaling the whole frame does not make small-object detection hard. It makes it impossible — the information is gone before the model is allowed to look.

This is the trap that swallows newcomers. They pick a good detector, train it well, and watch it score beautifully on large objects and blindly on small ones, and they blame the model. The model never had a chance. The pixels that carried the object were thrown away at the door.

8.2 Tiling: cut the frame, not the object

The fix is to stop shrinking. Instead of squeezing a huge frame down to fit a small input, we cut the huge frame into a grid of smaller pieces — each one at, or near, the detector's native input size — and run the detector on each piece independently. Then we take all the per-piece detections and translate them back into full-frame coordinates. This is tiling (also called sliced inference, and in one widely used formulation SAHI, for "Slicing Aided Hyper Inference"): detect on many native-resolution crops of one frame, then merge the results.

The magic is that scale is now preserved. Inside a tile, the detector sees the frame at full resolution. That twenty-pixel object stays twenty pixels. It is no longer competing for survival against a 4.4× downscale — it arrives at the network with every pixel it started with, on a background the network can actually study.

8.3 Overlap and the merge, where it goes wrong

A grid of clean, non-touching tiles has an obvious bug: an object sitting on a tile boundary gets sliced in half, and each half is a mangled fragment that neither tile detects well. The fix is overlap. Adjacent tiles share a band of pixels — the shaded seams in the figure — wide enough that any object smaller than the band is fully contained in at least one tile. Overlap is not optional; it is the price of not losing objects at the seams.

Overlap creates its own problem, and this is where tiling implementations quietly go wrong. An object in the shared band is seen by two tiles, so it is detected twice. After you translate both boxes into full-frame coordinates, you have two overlapping boxes for one real object. If you do nothing, your system reports a phantom pair where there is a single aircraft.

The cure is Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS): among a cluster of boxes that overlap heavily, keep the one with the highest confidence and suppress the rest. Run NMS on the merged, full-frame detections — after translation, not before — and the double detection collapses back to one. The merge, done correctly, is a three-step ritual, and every step is a place people get it wrong:

  1. Translate. A box detected at tile-local coordinates (x, y) becomes a full-frame box at (x + tile_origin_x, y + tile_origin_y). Forget the tile origin and every detection piles up in the top-left corner. This is the single most common tiling bug.
  2. Pool. Gather every translated box from every tile into one flat list against the full frame. Detections must compete globally, not within their tile.
  3. Suppress. Run NMS over the pooled list so overlap-region duplicates are removed and one object yields one box.
⚠ The subtle failure is silent

None of these mistakes throws an error. Skip the origin translation and you still get plausible boxes — just all crowded into one corner. Skip the global NMS and you still get detections — just doubled along every seam, which quietly inflates your false-positive count and wrecks the metric in Chapter 9. Tiling bugs do not crash; they lie. Verify the merge by eye on a frame with a known object straddling a boundary before you trust a single number.

8.4 Tile at train and eval, not just at deploy

Tiling is not a runtime trick you bolt on at the end. It is a property of the data the whole pipeline agrees on, and it must be applied consistently at all three stages — or the numbers you report will not mean what you think.

For training, you crop tiles around and near objects, so the detector learns on crops that look like the crops it will see at inference. For deployment, you run the full overlapping grid over each live frame. And for evaluation — this is the part teams skip — you must score on a deterministic, full-coverage tile grid over the whole frame, and you must keep the empty tiles.

That last instruction is the one that separates an honest metric from a flattering one. Most tiles in a real frame contain no object at all — they are pure sky, cloud, or terrain. Those empty tiles are precisely where false positives are born, because they are nothing but the adversarial background of Chapter 1. If your evaluation quietly drops the empty tiles and scores only on tiles that happen to contain an object, you have deleted every opportunity the model had to raise a false alarm. Your precision looks wonderful and your product does not exist. Keeping the empty tiles is the same matched-distribution discipline Chapter 7 used to cure its train/eval mismatch: evaluate on the distribution you will actually deploy on, seams, sky, and all.

8.5 The payoff: the product metric

Sliced inference over the whole frame, with full coverage and honest empty tiles, is not just a number — it is the number. It is the metric that reflects what the system does in deployment: take a real frame, tile it, detect, merge, and report. When a build quotes this figure, it is quoting the product.

In one build, the sliced whole-frame pipeline reached AP@50 around 0.922, with precision near 0.91 and recall near 0.92, and a false-positive rate around 0.095 per frame — roughly one false alarm for every ten full frames on normal flights. That is a deployable system. Contrast it with the naive whole-frame-downscale path from §8.1, which does not have a lower score on small objects so much as it has no ability to see them at all. The gap between those two numbers is the entire justification for the cost we are about to incur.

8.6 More pixels are not more signal

Here is the seductive next thought. If tiling helps by giving the object more pixels, then zooming in should help even more. Magnify the frame 2× and use smaller tiles — say 280 pixels — so each object is rendered across four times the area. Surely a target the network can see bigger is a target it can see better. It is a reasonable hypothesis. It is also wrong, and the way it is wrong is worth internalizing.

The 2× magnification that made things worse

Rejected

A team hypothesized that if tiling wins by adding pixels, more magnification would win harder. They rebuilt the pipeline at 2× magnification with smaller 280-pixel tiles, upsampling each object to roughly four times its native pixel count before the detector saw it. The result went the wrong way. AP@50 dropped — on the order of 0.835 against 0.857 for the plain tiling baseline — while the runtime cost rose by about 3.5×, because smaller tiles mean far more tiles per frame. They paid triple and got less.

Root cause. Magnification adds pixels but not signal-to-noise — the ratio of real object information to sensor noise and clutter. A blurry five-pixel smear upsampled to twenty pixels is still a five-pixel smear's worth of information. You have not recovered any detail that was not captured; you have interpolated between the pixels you had and added interpolation artifacts on top. Meanwhile the smaller tiles multiplied the tile count, and every extra tile is another patch of empty sky that can produce a false positive. The net was less truth at higher cost.

Tiling preserves the signal you captured. Magnification inflates the pixels around it. Only one of those is information — more tiles are not more truth.

8.7 The constraints you will actually hit

Two practical realities turn the clean idea of §8.2 into an engineering negotiation, and both deserve to be stated before they bite you.

✗ The stride constraint: your tile is not the size you think

Most detectors require input dimensions divisible by 32 — a consequence of the network downsampling the image by factors of two several times (its stride). A "560" tile is not divisible by 32; the nearest valid size up is 576. Get this wrong and the framework does one of two unhelpful things: it silently pads your tile to a legal size, shifting every coordinate and quietly corrupting the merge in §8.3, or it errors out mid-run. Choose tile sizes that are multiples of 32 on purpose, and know the real number, because the coordinate math depends on it.

⚠ Tiling multiplies inference cost

The whole-frame path is one forward pass per frame. Tiling is N forward passes for N tiles — a 3×3 grid is nine times the compute, plus overlap. On a workstation that is a shrug. On the vehicle, where Chapter 10 counts every millisecond and milliwatt, N is a hard budget line. The tile count is the most direct accuracy-versus-latency knob in the entire system: more, smaller tiles catch more tiny objects and cost more time; fewer, larger tiles are cheaper and start dropping the faint ones.

8.8 The trade-off, plainly

Tiling is the single most effective technique for small-object detection and the single most expensive thing your runtime does. Those two facts are inseparable. You do not get the 0.922 without paying for N forward passes, and you cannot pay for unlimited forward passes on a vehicle with a fixed power and latency budget. So tiling is not a setting you turn on; it is a set of dials you tune — tile size, overlap fraction, and tile count — against the object sizes you must catch and the milliseconds and milliwatts you have to spend.

The right operating point is not universal. A wildlife survey running offline on recorded footage can afford a dense grid and chase every faint bird. A collision-avoidance system on a small platform may have the budget for only a coarse grid and must accept that the very smallest, very faintest objects will slip through until they grow. That choice is a system decision in exactly the sense Chapter 1 warned about — and, like everything else in this book, it is a decision about the pipeline, not the model. The pipeline is the product.

Takeaways
  • Downscaling destroys the object. Fitting a 2448-px frame into a 560-px input shrinks a 20-px target to 4–5 px — below resolvability — before the network ever runs. That is the resolution mismatch, and it is fatal.
  • Tiling cuts the frame, not the object. Detect on native-resolution tiles, then merge to full-frame coordinates. The object keeps every pixel it started with.
  • Overlap, then merge carefully. Overlap so boundary objects stay whole; then translate by tile origin, pool globally, and de-duplicate with NMS. These bugs are silent — verify by eye.
  • Evaluate on the deploy distribution. Use a deterministic full-coverage grid and keep the empty tiles — they are where false positives live. Dropping them fakes the score.
  • Magnification is not free SNR. More pixels are not more signal; a 2× zoom once dropped AP@50 at 3.5× the cost. More tiles are not more truth.
  • Tile count is the accuracy-vs-latency knob. N tiles ≈ N forward passes; mind the ÷32 stride constraint, and tune tile size, overlap, and count against the budget you carry on the vehicle.
