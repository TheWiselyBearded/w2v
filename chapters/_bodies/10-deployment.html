Part IV · Chapter 10

Off the Desktop — surviving the crossing to the edge.

A model that works in the luxurious offline plane must now survive the austere runtime plane on the vehicle. This chapter is the single export step that connects the two, and everything that goes wrong in the crossing. On the edge the currency is milliseconds and milliwatts, not mean average precision.

In Chapter 2 we drew the system as two planes. The offline plane is where the model was born: a big GPU, unlimited time, gigabytes of memory, and permission to be slow if slow means accurate. The runtime plane is where it has to live: a small embedded accelerator bolted to a flying platform, with hard limits on latency, memory, and power, and no adult supervision. Those two worlds meet at exactly one place — the export step — and that seam is where more good models die than anywhere else in the book.

This chapter is about the crossing. Not about training a better detector; you did that already. About taking the detector you have and getting it, intact and honest, onto hardware that was never as forgiving as your workstation. Almost nothing that follows is a modeling decision. It is a systems decision, and treating it as anything else is how teams ship a model that scored well on Friday and produced garbage in flight on Monday.

10.1 The interchange boundary: ONNX

You train in one framework — say PyTorch — but you must not deploy in it. The runtime on the vehicle should not depend on your training framework, its Python interpreter, its version churn, or its assumptions about having a desktop GPU. So between training and deployment we insert a neutral format. The standard one is ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange): an open, framework-neutral description of a trained model's computation graph — the sequence of operations and weights that turn an input tensor into an output tensor, written down in a way that many different runtimes can read.

You export your trained model to ONNX once, and from that point the runtime speaks only ONNX. This is the same move we made for data in Chapter 2, where every source was translated into one canonical record at the door. Here every model is translated into one canonical graph before it crosses to the edge. ONNX is the narrow waist for models: many training frameworks on one side, many hardware runtimes on the other, and a single interchange format at the pinch point so neither side has to know about the other.

◇ Why a graph, not a checkpoint

A training checkpoint is a bag of weights that only means something to the exact code that produced it. An ONNX graph is self-describing: it carries the operations, their connectivity, and the weights together, so a runtime that has never seen your training code can execute it. That self-description is exactly what lets the runtime plane stay small and framework-free — and it is also what makes the next section necessary, because not every runtime supports every operation the exporter chose to write down.

10.2 Export parity is not free

Here is the assumption everyone makes and no one should: that the exported model computes the same function as the trained one. It often does not. Operators get approximated or substituted because the target lacks an exact equivalent. Tensor memory layouts differ. And most treacherous of all, preprocessing that was implicit in your training code — a resize here, a normalization there, buried in a data-loader nobody thinks of as part of the model — must be reimplemented from scratch in the runtime, by hand, often by a different person in a different language.

So we make export parity a promotion gate, exactly like the gates in Chapter 2. The rule is mechanical: run the trained model and the exported model on the same inputs, assert their outputs match within a small numerical tolerance, and fail the promotion if they diverge. Not "investigate later." Fail. A model that cannot prove it survived its own export has not earned the right to be measured on-device, let alone flown.

The model that scored well and saw garbage

what broke

A detector passed every offline check with room to spare — strong on the real holdout, tidy false-alarm rate, small objects caught. It was exported, quantized, and loaded onto the target board. On-device it was a disaster: boxes in the wrong places, confident detections on empty sky, real aircraft missed entirely. The weights were bit-for-bit the ones that had worked. Same model, same graph. Different pixels.

The offline data-loader had resized frames with one interpolation method and normalized them in a particular order — subtract mean, then scale — as an incidental detail nobody had written down. The runtime engineer, reimplementing preprocessing in C++ against the camera path, used a different resize interpolation and applied the normalization in the opposite order. Every pixel entering the network was subtly wrong, so every feature the network computed was subtly wrong, and a detector tuned to twenty-pixel specks has no margin for "subtly wrong."

The fix: pin and test the entire preprocessing chain across the boundary, not just the weights. The parity gate was extended to feed identical raw frames through both the offline and the runtime preprocessing and assert the resulting tensors matched before either reached the network. The boundary object is not the model — it is the model plus every pixel transformation in front of it. Once the whole chain was pinned and tested, on-device output matched offline output, and it stayed matched.

The weights are the easy part. What crosses the boundary is the whole function — every resize, every normalization, every layout — and if you only test the weights you have only tested the easy part.

10.3 Quantization: buying speed with precision

The trained model stores its weights and computes its activations in 32-bit floating point (FP32) — luxurious, accurate, and far heavier than the edge wants to carry. Quantization is the process of converting those numbers to lower precision — most often 8-bit integers (INT8) — so the model is smaller in memory and dramatically faster on hardware that has dedicated integer acceleration. The wins are real and large: a fraction of the memory footprint, a multiple of the throughput, less power drawn per frame.

It is not free either. To map a wide range of float values onto a narrow range of integers, the quantizer needs to know what ranges actually occur — so it needs calibration: you run a batch of representative data through the model and record the real distribution of values at each layer, then choose the integer mapping that preserves them best. Calibrate on unrepresentative data and you clip or crush exactly the values that mattered.

Because quantization changes the numbers the model computes, it can change what the model decides — and small objects, living in a handful of low-contrast pixels, are precisely the signals a coarse integer grid is most likely to lose. A quantization regression — accuracy that silently drops after quantizing — is a real, named failure tag, and the only defense is to re-measure. Not assume, not eyeball. After quantizing, run the quantized model against the frozen benchmark from Chapter 9 and compare it to the FP32 baseline on the metrics that matter, especially small-object recall and false-alarm rate. If the regression exceeds tolerance, the model does not ship.

▸ FP16: the milder middle ground

Quantization is not all-or-nothing. Between FP32 and INT8 sits 16-bit floating point (FP16), which halves memory and speeds up on hardware with float16 units while staying far closer to the original numerics than INT8. It is a common first step: take the easy win of FP16, measure, and only push to INT8 if the latency budget still demands it. The discipline is identical — every precision change is a change to the function, and every change gets re-measured on the frozen benchmark.

10.4 Measure on the device, not the desktop

Your desktop's inference speed is not a proxy for the vehicle's. Different chip, different memory bandwidth, different supported operators, different thermal reality. The only measurements that mean anything are taken on the actual target hardware, running the full pipeline — preprocess, detector, tracker, postprocess — not just the model's forward pass in isolation. And you do not measure the average. Averages hide the tail, and the tail is what drops frames.

So we measure latency percentiles: p50, p95, and p99 — the time under which 50%, 95%, and 99% of frames complete. A comfortable average with an ugly p99 means one frame in a hundred blows the budget, stutters the tracker, and loses the object you were following. We also measure throughput (frames per second), peak memory, and — the one desktops never reveal — sustained thermal behavior. A chip that hits latency for ten seconds and then throttles under its own heat is a chip that fails in the field, where it runs for the whole flight, not for a demo.

10.5 The hardware is a constraint you design around

⚠ Choose the silicon before you choose the model

The target system-on-chip or accelerator, its SDK, its supported operators, its precision modes, its memory and power envelope, and the camera path that feeds it — all of these should be decided early, because together they constrain which models you can even consider. If your model uses an operator the accelerator does not implement, that op falls back to the CPU — slow, power-hungry, sometimes catastrophic for latency — or forces a redesign. A detector that is two percent more accurate but leans on one unsupported operator may simply be undeployable. Accuracy you cannot run is not accuracy.

This inverts the naive order of operations. It is tempting to pick the best model on the benchmark and worry about hardware later. On the edge, hardware is not "later" — it is a boundary condition on the whole design. The supported-operator list of your accelerator is as much a part of your model's specification as its layer count. Read it before you commit to an architecture, not after you have trained one.

10.6 Tiling collides with the budget

Recall the small-object machinery from Chapter 8: to keep twenty-pixel objects alive, we run the detector on tiles — cropped sub-frames at higher effective resolution — rather than on one shrunken whole image. It works. It is also the single most expensive thing you can do to your latency budget, because inference cost multiplies by the number of tiles. Nine tiles is, to first order, nine forward passes per frame.

This is the cruel symmetry of the problem. The exact technique you need most for small objects is the technique that most strains the edge. So tile count, input resolution, model size, and numeric precision are not four separate choices — they are four knobs on a single accuracy-vs- latency-vs-power trade surface. Turn up tiles for recall and you spend latency and power. Drop to INT8 to buy it back and you risk a quantization regression on the very objects the tiles were protecting. Shrink the model and you may lose the small objects directly.

Deployment, then, is not "make the model run." It is choosing a defensible point on that surface, and defending it with numbers measured on the target. That is why this chapter belongs to systems engineering as much as to machine learning.

10.7 The runtime plane, briefly

What actually runs on the vehicle is deliberately, almost boringly simple — and that simplicity is a safety feature, the boundary from Chapter 2 §2.6 made physical. The runtime path is a straight line: frame → preprocess → detector → tracker → detections → log or display. It ingests images and emits detections and tracks with their confidences. It does not decide what to do with them. There is no branch in that line where perception becomes action, because the output contract has no field for action to fill.

Keeping the runtime plane perception-only is what lets the same deployed pipeline serve a delivery drone's sense-and-avoid, an airport's airspace monitor, or a wildlife survey, without change — and it is what keeps the edge software small enough to reason about, test, and trust. The line is short on purpose. Every box you do not add to it is a box that cannot fail in flight.

10.8 Across the seam

The crossing has a shape now. Export through ONNX so the runtime never depends on your training framework. Gate on parity so the exported function — weights and preprocessing — matches the trained one bit for bit within tolerance. Quantize for the speed and memory the edge demands, then re-measure on the frozen benchmark so a quantization regression cannot hide. Measure latency percentiles, throughput, memory, and sustained thermals on the real device, across the whole pipeline. And choose your point on the accuracy-vs-latency-vs-power surface with the hardware's supported operators and the tiling budget both in view from the start.

A model that survives all of that is a model that finally does something the offline plane never could: it runs in the world and generates real footage of real skies, including real failures the simulator never imagined. That footage is not the end of the pipeline. It is the beginning of the next turn of the loop — how a deployed system feeds its own failures back into the curation and training it came from. That is Chapter 11.

Takeaways
  • One seam connects two planes. Training's luxury and the vehicle's austerity meet at a single export step; treat that crossing as a systems problem, not a modeling afterthought.
  • ONNX is the narrow waist for models. Export to a neutral graph so the runtime never depends on your training framework — the same discipline the canonical record gave the data.
  • Gate on export parity, including preprocessing. Assert the exported model matches the trained one within tolerance on identical inputs — and pin the entire pixel chain, because same weights with different pixels is garbage on-device.
  • Quantize, then re-measure. INT8 buys speed and memory for a usually-small accuracy cost, but a quantization regression on small objects is real — verify on the frozen benchmark or do not ship.
  • Measure on the device, at the tail. p50/p95/p99 latency, throughput, peak memory, and sustained thermals — on the target, across the full pipeline. The p99 is the honest budget.
  • Tiling, resolution, model size, and precision are one trade surface. Deployment is choosing a point on it that clears accuracy, latency, and thermal limits together — argued with device measurements, not preference.
