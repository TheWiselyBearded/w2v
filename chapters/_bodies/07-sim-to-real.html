Part III · Chapter 7

The Sim-to-Real Gap — where the reckoning finally comes due.

Part II built abundant, perfectly-labeled synthetic skies. This chapter is the moment we point the resulting model at real footage and find out what it is actually worth. The first honest number is brutal — and it is the most important number in the book, because it is the one everything after it improves against.

Everything until now has been construction. We built a canonical record, a simulator, a way to turn geometry into labels, and a detector that scores beautifully on the worlds we rendered. It is a satisfying place to be, and it is a trap. A model that works on synthetic imagery has not been shown to work; it has been shown to work on synthetic imagery. Those are different claims, and the distance between them is the subject of this chapter and the hardest, most important work in the book.

Give the distance its name. The sim-to-real gap — also called the domain gap — is the drop in performance you suffer when a model trained on one distribution of images (simulated) is asked to perform on a different distribution (real). The model has not gotten worse; the world has changed underneath it. The pixels it learned to trust are not the pixels it now sees, and it fails in ways that are, at first, mysterious. The rest of this chapter is about making them un-mysterious: measure the gap, diagnose its causes, and close it.

7.1 You cannot fix what you refuse to measure

The temptation, when you have poured months into a synthetic pipeline, is to keep admiring the synthetic scores. Resist it. The only number that matters is performance on real imagery the model has never seen. So we do the uncomfortable thing first: train on synthetic data (plus whatever small amount of real data we have), then evaluate on a held-out set of real flights that never touched training. This is the real holdout — the gate from Chapter 2 that exists precisely because someone was once fooled by brilliant synthetic scores that evaporated on real footage.

Here is what that first honest evaluation produced in one build. The headline metric is mean average precision (mAP): a single number from 0 to 1 summarizing how well the detector's boxes match the truth across confidence thresholds. On synthetic data it looked excellent. On real footage:

0.021mAP on real footageessentially not working — the honest baseline
~0.9on syntheticthe score that fooled everyone into feeling done
≈40×the illusionhow much better it looked than it was
0.000of a target reachedthe number to beat, not the number to mourn

Twenty-one thousandths. On real footage the model was, for all practical purposes, blind. It is tempting to read that as catastrophic failure — months of work, and the thing does not see. Read it the other way. That 0.021 is not a verdict; it is a baseline. It is the first point on a curve you are about to push upward, and you could not have started climbing until you knew where the ground was. A team that never runs this evaluation ships the 0.9 and discovers the 0.021 in the field, where it costs far more than pride.

A brutal honest number is worth more than a beautiful dishonest one. The synthetic score told you the pipeline runs; the real score tells you whether it works.

7.2 Four reasons the sky was a stranger

A single scalar tells you that you have a problem; it does not tell you what the problem is. The next move is diagnosis — pulling up the actual failure images and asking, frame by frame, why the model is wrong. In this build the 0.021 was not one bug. It was four distinct distribution mismatches stacked on top of one another, and each had a different fix. Lamenting the gap is useless. Decomposing it is everything.

Root causeSymptom on real footageFix
No ground clutter. Synthetic scenes were empty sky; real takeoff and low-altitude frames are full of terrain. A false-positive storm. The model never learned that a ridgeline isn't an object, so it flags mountainside rocks, buildings, and treelines by the dozen. Render terrain, horizons, and ground clutter into the synthetic worlds — the exact warning raised back in Chapter 3. Teach "not an object" on purpose.
Resolution mismatch. Real sensor frames are enormous (e.g. 2448×2048) and get downscaled before the network sees them. Tiny objects vanish. Downscaling a 2448-wide frame to 560 px shrinks a 20-px target to about 4 px — below the size at which the detector can respond at all. Stop downscaling the object away. Feed the network native-resolution tiles instead of a shrunken whole frame — the full machinery of Chapter 8.
Class / label mismatch. Synthetic labels used specific classes; the real dataset labeled most objects as one generic "unknown / airborne" class. The metric punished correct work. A perfectly localized box was scored wrong because its predicted class didn't match a label that never distinguished classes anyway. Evaluate class-agnostic when the taxonomies don't line up: ask "did you find the object at all," not "did you name it." (More in Chapter 9.)
Appearance mismatch. Real footage was grayscale, noisy, and compressed; synthetic was clean RGB. The model keyed on cues that don't exist in real frames — color, crisp edges — and stumbled on sensor grain and compression blocks it had never encountered. Augment training to cover the real look: randomly convert to grayscale, add sensor noise, blur, and compression artifacts so the training distribution reaches into the real one.

Notice what this table is not: a list of things the model did badly. It is a list of things the data failed to represent. Three of the four fixes change what enters the pipeline — render clutter, tile at native resolution, augment appearance — and the fourth changes how you measure. None of them touches the network architecture. That is the thesis of the book arriving exactly on schedule: the pipeline is the product, and the gap lives in the pipeline, not the model.

⚠ The false-positive storm is the loudest failure, not the deepest

When terrain enters the frame, a clutter-naive model can invent dozens of detections per frame. It is dramatic, and it dominates the first look at the failure images. But it is also the easiest of the four to fix — you simply have to show the model terrain during training. Do not let the loudest symptom convince you it is the whole disease. The resolution and distribution problems below are quieter and cost more to close.

7.3 The deepest cut: train and eval must match

The four causes above are mismatches between synthetic and real. There is a subtler, more expensive mismatch that lives entirely inside your own pipeline, and it is worth stopping on because it is the kind of mistake a competent team makes precisely because they are trying to do the right thing.

The redesign that trained on one world and was graded on another

what broke

A promising redesign set out to solve the small-object problem the honest way: instead of feeding the network downscaled whole frames, it trained on tight crops taken right around the objects, so every training image was mostly target and the tiny-object signal was preserved. On paper, exactly right. But the evaluation was left as it had always been: downscaled whole frames. So the model now trained on one distribution — zoomed-in crops, dense with objects — and was judged on a completely different one — shrunken full scenes, mostly empty sky. It had never, not once, seen at training time the kind of image it was being graded on.

The result was a slow-motion disappointment. Validation performance limped along, peaked somewhere around 0.010 mAP, and the run was killed early as a clear dead end. The instinct in the room was to blame the model — wrong architecture, wrong hyperparameters, wrong idea. All of that was innocent. The model was fine. What was broken was that what it learned from and what it was measured on were no longer the same thing. A student drilled on one exam and tested on another fails, and the failure says nothing about the student.

cropswhat it trained on
whole frameswhat it was graded on
~0.010where it peaked before it was killed
0bugs in the model itself

The fix is not a better model; it is a matched distribution. Make evaluation use the same tiling the deployment will use: a deterministic, full-coverage grid of tiles cut at native resolution — and, crucially, keep the empty tiles. It is tempting to drop tiles that contain no object, because they seem like wasted computation, but the empty tiles are where the model learns and is tested on its ability to say "nothing here." Discard them and you both starve training of negatives and inflate your score by never grading the model on the clutter it will actually face. This matched-distribution, full-grid evaluation is the SAHI-style approach we build in Chapter 8 and measure honestly in Chapter 9.

Train and evaluation data must be drawn from the same distribution — and that distribution should be the one you will actually deploy in. Optimize anything else and you will optimize a fiction.

This is the same principle that governed the whole architecture in Chapter 2, now aimed at the experiment itself: decide what "the real task" looks like, and make every stage — training, validation, and the deployed runtime — agree on that one shape.

7.4 What actually closed the gap: more world, not more model

With the four causes diagnosed and the train/eval distributions aligned, we had a system that could improve. The question was which lever moved it most. We tried the usual suspects — bigger backbones, longer schedules, cleverer losses — and they helped at the margins. But the single biggest lever, by a wide margin, was embarrassingly simple: more real data, and more diverse real data.

Scaling the real training set from a couple dozen flights to a few hundred flights — the same model, the same recipe, just a wider and more varied slice of the actual world — moved the numbers like nothing else did:

Metric~2 dozen flights~few hundred flightsChange
AP@50 (overall)0.7750.857+0.082
Small-object recall (16–32 px bin)——+0.16

Read the last row carefully, because it is the surprising one. More data usually buys you diminishing returns and eventually an overfitting cliff, where the model starts memorizing and generalization falls. At this level of diversity, no such cliff appeared — the model kept getting better as the world it saw got wider. Diversity, not just volume, is what buys generalization: two hundred flights that are all the same clear afternoon over the same valley teach less than fifty flights across weather, terrain, light, and sensor.

And notice, once more, what did the work. The architecture between the 0.775 build and the 0.857 build was essentially unchanged. The data changed. This is the entire argument of the book compressed into two numbers: you do not climb the sim-to-real gap primarily by inventing a better detector. You climb it by feeding a competent detector a richer, more honest, more varied picture of the world it will actually fly in.

✓ The lever with the best return

Before you spend a week on a novel architecture, ask whether a week spent collecting and curating more diverse real footage would move the number further. In this campaign it almost always would. The model is the cheap part; the data is the product.

7.5 Overfitting to synthetic, and the cheapest insurance against it

There is a specific failure mode that stalks any training mix heavy on synthetic data. Because synthetic imagery is clean and internally consistent, a network can memorize its quirks fast — the particular way the renderer draws an edge, the exact statistics of its noise-free sky — and briefly look excellent, before that memorization actively hurts it on real frames. In one run, validation performance peaked at epoch 2 of 12 and then declined for the remaining ten epochs as the model overfit the synthetic look. Left to run to the end, the last checkpoint was worse than one it had passed through early.

There are two responses, and you want both.

First, promote the best checkpoint, not the last one. Track validation on the real holdout every epoch and keep the weights from the epoch that scored highest. If the model diverges later, that divergence costs you nothing — you already saved the good one. This is cheap, universal insurance, and it should be on by default.

Second — and this is the real fix — address the mix. Train fewer epochs, and rebalance the synthetic-to-real ratio so the model is not given the time or the incentive to memorize synthetic quirks in the first place. Best-checkpoint promotion makes overfitting cheap to tolerate; it does not make it go away. If your best checkpoint is always epoch 2, that is not a happy accident to exploit forever — it is the pipeline telling you the training mix is wrong.

✕ A peak at epoch 2 is a symptom, not a strategy

If validation reliably peaks almost immediately and then falls, resist the urge to simply set epochs=2 and call it solved. That treats the thermometer instead of the fever. The early peak means the synthetic-heavy mix is teaching the model to memorize rather than generalize; fix the mix, and the peak moves to where it belongs.

7.6 Pilot first: never bet a night on an unvalidated idea

Everything in this chapter — rendering clutter, native-resolution tiling, class-agnostic evaluation, appearance augmentation, distribution matching, data scaling — is a change to the pipeline, and every change is a hypothesis that could be wrong. A full training run is expensive: hours of GPU time, often an overnight commitment, and a full day lost if the idea was flawed. Discovering at 9 a.m. that last night's redesign trained on the wrong distribution is a bad way to spend a day.

So before committing to the expensive run, run a cheap pilot: a small, fast dry run that validates the direction before you scale it. Hash-gate a subsample of the data — say 15%, selected deterministically by hashing each sample's identifier so the subset is stable and reproducible — and train a few epochs, about an hour, with identical data and evaluation settings to the full run. The pilot will not give you your final number, but it will tell you whether the curve is pointing the right way. Only scale what the pilot shows is working; kill what it shows is not, before it costs you a night.

The pilot-first habit is the experimental discipline that makes the rest of this chapter affordable. The gap is closed by many small, honest experiments, and you can only afford many experiments if each one is cheap to be wrong about.

7.7 Where the gap stands

We began this chapter at 0.021 — a model that scored beautifully on synthetic imagery and was, on real footage, effectively blind. We did not close that gap with a better network. We closed it by measuring the gap honestly, decomposing it into four nameable causes with four different fixes, aligning what we trained on with what we deployed and measured, and then — above all — feeding the model a wider, more diverse picture of the real world. The model barely changed. The data, and the discipline around it, changed completely. That is the whole book in one chapter.

Two threads ran through everything here and are owed their own chapters. The tiny-object machinery this chapter kept deferring to — tiling, native resolution, full-coverage grids that keep their empty tiles — is the subject of Chapter 8. And the honest measurement that made every number in this chapter trustworthy — class-agnostic evaluation, small-object bins, false-alarm rate, matched distributions — is the subject of Chapter 9, where we learn how measuring the wrong thing can make a good detector look broken and a broken one look fine.

Takeaways
  • Measure before you fix. The first honest real-footage number — 0.021 mAP here — is not a failure but a baseline. You cannot close a gap you have never quantified.
  • Decompose the gap into causes. Ours was four stacked mismatches — no ground clutter, resolution downscaling, class/label mismatch, and appearance — each with its own fix, three of them in the data and one in the metric.
  • Train and eval must share a distribution — the deployed one. A redesign that trained on crops but was graded on whole frames peaked at ~0.010 and was killed; the model was innocent, the mismatch was fatal. Use the same tiling everywhere, and keep the empty tiles.
  • More diverse real data is the biggest lever. Scaling from a couple dozen to a few hundred flights moved AP@50 from 0.775 to 0.857 with no overfitting cliff — the model barely changed, the data did.
  • Promote the best checkpoint and fix the mix; pilot before you commit. Best-checkpoint promotion makes synthetic overfitting cheap to tolerate but never fixes it; a 15%, one-hour pilot with identical settings protects an overnight run from a wrong idea.
