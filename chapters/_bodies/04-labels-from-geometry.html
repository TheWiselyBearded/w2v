Part II · Chapter 4

Truth From Geometry — labeling a world you already know.

In a simulator you never have to guess where an object is — you placed it there. This chapter builds a labeling pipeline that computes each box directly from 3D geometry, shows why those labels are renderer-independent, and tells the story of a bug that was invisible precisely because everything agreed with everything else.

In Chapter 3 we built worlds: a camera flying through a scene full of aircraft, drones, and birds, with a render engine turning that scene into pixels. Now we need the other half of a training example — the label. For every frame, we need a tight box around every object, its class, its identity, and how visible it is, in exactly the canonical shape Chapter 2 demanded. The good news, and the whole reason we built our own worlds in the first place, is that in a simulator the answer is not hidden. The computer knows the precise 3D position of every object and the exact pose of the camera. The label is not something to be discovered. It is something to be computed.

4.1 Two ways to get a label

There are two fundamentally different ways to produce a box in a simulated scene, and the choice between them shapes everything that follows.

The first is to scrape the label from the renderer. Modern render engines can be asked, for each pixel, which object it belongs to — a so-called segmentation or instance pass. Collect all the pixels tagged with a given object, take their bounding rectangle, and you have a box. This is easy and it is exact for what the renderer drew.

The second is to compute the label analytically from geometry. You know the object's position in the world. You know where the camera is and which way it points. So you do the projection yourself, with arithmetic: take the object's 3D extent, push it through a camera model, and read off the pixel box. No render pass required.

The analytic path has one property that makes it the backbone of this chapter: it is renderer-independent — it depends only on the scene geometry, not on which engine drew the picture. The identical labeling code produces the identical box whether the frame was rendered by Unity, by NVIDIA Omniverse, or by a mock renderer that draws nothing but a dot. That is the canonical-record narrow waist from Chapter 2 reaching into the labeler: a source adapter can hand our geometry to one labeling function and get back canonical labels, no matter what backend produced the imagery.

4.2 A pinhole camera, from scratch

To project a point ourselves we need a camera model, and the simplest one that works is the pinhole camera: an idealized camera where all light passes through a single point, so the world maps onto the image by straight lines. It has two groups of numbers. The extrinsics are the camera's pose in the world — where it is (a position) and which way it faces (an orientation). The intrinsics are the camera's internal optics — its field of view (equivalently, its focal length) and its principal point, the pixel the optical axis passes through.

Projecting one 3D world point to one pixel is a short sequence of steps. We will do it in a right-handed world with the screen's y-axis pointing down — a convention we must state out loud, because §4.5 is the story of what happens when you don't.

   WORLD SPACE                         CAMERA SPACE            IMAGE

       /                                    /                      │  ·P'│

   step 4: map to viewport 0..1, then →  pixel = viewport × resolution

In code the sequence is almost as short as the diagram:

project one world point to a normalized viewport coordinate

def project(p_world, cam):
    # 1. translate: point relative to the camera
    rel = p_world - cam.position
    # 2. rotate into the camera's right/up/forward basis
    x = dot(rel, cam.right)
    y = dot(rel, cam.up)
    z = dot(rel, cam.forward)      # camera-space depth
    # 3. CULL anything at or behind the lens
    if z <= 0:
        return None                # object is behind the camera
    # 4. perspective divide, then map to a 0..1 viewport
    u = 0.5 + (x / z) / (2 * tan(cam.h_fov / 2))
    v = 0.5 - (y / z) / (2 * tan(cam.v_fov / 2))   # y-down screen
    return (u, v)                  # multiply by resolution LATER
◇ Why we stop at a 0..1 viewport

Notice the function returns numbers between 0 and 1, not pixels. That is deliberate. If you bake the resolution into the projection, a label made at 720p is wrong at 2448px and you will re-derive the same box for every output size. Keep the geometry in normalized viewport coordinates — fractions of the frame — and multiply by the resolution only at the very end. The same scene then labels identically at any resolution, which is exactly what a pipeline that renders the same world at many sizes needs.

4.3 From a point to a box: amodal, modal, and the edges of the frame

An object is not a point; it has extent. Project the eight corners of its 3D bounding volume, take the minimum and maximum of the resulting viewport coordinates, and you have a 2D box. But which box? There are two, and confusing them corrupts training data quietly.

The amodal box is the rectangle around the object's full extent, including the parts you cannot see — the piece hidden behind a cloud, or the wing that runs off the edge of the frame. The modal box is the rectangle around only the visible part. For a distant aircraft peeking past a ridgeline, the amodal box says "here is the whole aircraft"; the modal box says "here is the sliver of it you can actually see."

For our small, distant targets the amodal box is usually what you want to learn from — it is the honest answer to "where is the object" — but you record visibility alongside it so a trainer can decide for itself. Two edge cases must be handled with care, because a target that misbehaves should degrade cleanly rather than poison the dataset:

  • Clip to the viewport. An amodal box may extend past the frame edge. Clamp its pixel coordinates to the image bounds so a consumer never sees a box hanging in negative space — while remembering that the underlying object was larger.
  • Cull what is behind the camera. A point with non-positive camera-space depth (z ≤ 0) is behind the lens. The perspective divide would happily produce a finite, completely wrong pixel for it — a mirror-image ghost. The labeler must drop such objects, so a target that overflies the camera and passes behind it simply disappears from the labels instead of leaving a phantom box on the far side of the sky.

4.4 How much can you actually see? Occlusion by ray sampling

The canonical record carries a visible_fraction field, and now we have to fill it honestly. Geometry alone tells us where an object would appear; it does not tell us whether a cloud, a hill, or another aircraft is in the way. To measure real visibility we sample.

The idea: cast a spray of rays from the camera toward points across the object's silhouette. For each ray, ask two questions. Does it hit this object's own collider (is the ray aimed at the object at all)? And does it reach the object first when tested against the whole scene, or does something else block it? The fraction of silhouette rays that survive the full-scene test — hitting our object before anything else — is the visible fraction.

visibility as a ratio of surviving rays

hits_object = 0     # rays that strike the object in isolation
hits_first  = 0     # rays that reach the object before any occluder
for ray in silhouette_rays(object, cam):
    if raycast(ray, object_only_collider):
        hits_object += 1
        if raycast(ray, full_scene).first_hit is object:
            hits_first += 1
visible_fraction = hits_first / max(hits_object, 1)

Then apply thresholds. Above a high bar — say 0.95 — call the object fully visible. Below a small floor — say 0.03 — treat it as effectively invisible, and in modal labeling drop a fully-occluded target entirely rather than emit a box around a thing you cannot see. Those exact numbers are not laws of nature; they are project configuration, tuned to how your trainer treats partially-hidden objects, and — like the promotion-gate thresholds in Chapter 2 — they belong in a config file, not buried in code.

4.5 The bug that agreed with itself

The label and the picture matched perfectly. Both were upside down.

What broke: a self-consistent flip

Building the camera's basis vectors, someone set the up-vector as up = cross(right, forward). With the handedness in play, that cross product did not point up. It pointed down. Every projection was therefore vertically flipped: objects in the top of the world landed in the bottom of the image.

Here is why it survived for so long. The same flawed basis was used to build both the analytic labels and the mock renderer that drew the test imagery. So the labels and the pictures were flipped in exactly the same way — and therefore agreed with each other perfectly. Every debug overlay showed a box sitting neatly on its object. Every internal consistency check — "does the label land where the render drew the dot?" — passed with flying colors. The pipeline was radiantly, confidently wrong.

It was caught only by a test that refused to consult the pipeline's own conventions. The test asserted the screen axes from the outside: place a world point directly above the camera; it must land in the upper half of the image, and screen-y must increase downward. That single ground-truth anchor — right-handed world, y-down screen, stated independently of any code the pipeline used — failed instantly and pointed straight at the inverted up-vector.

Agreement between two things you built the same way proves consistency, not correctness. You need at least one test anchored to ground truth outside your own conventions.

This is the most important lesson in the chapter, so it is worth saying without metaphor. When the labeler and the renderer share code, share conventions, or share an author, they will share their mistakes — and every check that compares one to the other is blind to exactly those mistakes. The only cure is an absolute test: an assertion about the world that does not route through the machinery under test. "Up is up" sounds too trivial to write down. It was the one thing that could have been written down.

4.6 The other path: renderer-derived labels

Analytic labels are not the only option, and sometimes they are not the best one. Modern synthetic-data tools — NVIDIA Omniverse Replicator's annotators are the reference example — can emit boxes directly from the render, and they can give you both flavors at once: a loose (amodal) box around the whole object and a tight (visible) box around just the pixels that survived occlusion. When photoreal fidelity matters and you want a pixel-accurate visible mask that reflects exactly what the sensor saw, this is hard to beat with arithmetic.

It has its own hazards, and each one has a defensive response:

  • One object is many meshes. A single aircraft is often a hierarchy of child meshes — fuselage, wings, gear — each of which the annotator may box separately. You must union the per-mesh boxes back into one object box, or you will train on fragments of aircraft.
  • Visibility is sometimes unknown. When the annotator cannot report occlusion, fall back to an area ratio — the tight box's area over the loose box's — as a rough visible fraction rather than emitting nothing.
  • Fail closed. If the annotator data is missing or malformed, the labeler must abort the frame, not shrug and emit a best guess. This is the Chapter 2 rule again: a malformed record must never travel one step deeper than where it was born. A silently bad label is worse than a missing one.
✓ Keep the analytic projection as a per-frame QA check

Here is the trick that ties both paths together. Even when you trust the renderer's labels, keep the analytic projection running alongside them, and emit their agreement — for instance the mean IoU (intersection-over-union: overlap area divided by union area, 1.0 for a perfect match) between the analytic box and the annotator box on every frame. Mean IoU ≈ 1 proves your camera convention and your label plumbing are correct. Mean IoU ≈ 0 means your pose math is wrong — it is exactly the alarm that would have screamed on the first frame of the flipped-vector bug. It is a cheap check with a huge blast radius, and it costs one extra projection per object.

4.7 Which path, and why it doesn't matter downstream

So: analytic or renderer-derived? Prefer analytic when you want the same labels across many rendering backends, resolution independence for free, and a labeler simple enough to test against absolute ground truth. Prefer renderer-derived when photoreal fidelity and exact, pixel-accurate visible masks matter more than cross-backend identity — when the picture is the product and you want labels that match its every pixel.

The quiet payoff of Chapter 2 is that a consumer never has to know which you chose. Both paths empty into the same canonical record — a box, a class, an identity, a visible fraction — and everything downstream reads only that. The labeler is one more piece of the pipeline, and the pipeline is the product: build it so its labels are correct against the world itself, not merely consistent with the picture next to them, and every stage after it inherits truth instead of inheriting your assumptions. In Chapter 5 we take these labeled frames and freeze them into the immutable, content-addressed datasets that make a result something you can trust rather than something you happen to remember.

Takeaways
  • Compute labels from geometry, don't just scrape them. You placed the objects and you know the camera, so project them yourself — analytic labels are renderer-independent and identical across Unity, Omniverse, or a mock backend.
  • Project in a normalized viewport, scale at the end. Keep the math in 0..1 fractions and multiply by resolution last, so the same scene labels identically at 720p and 2448px.
  • Know your box. Amodal is the full extent, modal is the visible part; clip to the viewport, cull anything behind the camera, and record visibility by ray-sampling so nothing degrades into a phantom.
  • Self-consistency is not correctness. A bug shared by your labeler and your renderer is invisible to every check that compares them. Anchor at least one test to ground truth outside your own conventions.
  • Run the analytic projection as QA even when you trust the renderer. Mean IoU between analytic and annotator boxes near 1 proves the pose math; near 0 catches the flip on frame one.
